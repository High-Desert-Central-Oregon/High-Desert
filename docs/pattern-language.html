A Pattern Language for Civic Infrastructure

After Christopher Alexander. Each design decision written as a pattern — the problem, the forces in tension, the resolving solution, and the evidence behind it. This is the form that lets the design travel to other communities, and to Warm Springs, without being imposed: patterns to adopt, adapt, or refuse.

How to read this

A pattern names a recurring problem and a way to resolve the forces around it. Patterns connect: each ends with the patterns it leads to. None is a rule — Alexander's insight is that mētis becomes transmissible only when you keep the reasoning (problem → forces → evidence) attached to the answer, so another community can re-decide it in their own context.

∗∗  high confidence — empirically grounded, load-bearing ∗  moderate — sound but to be tested in the field ·  tentative — a working hypothesis

I

Membership & Boundaries

Who belongs, how they enter, and how the edges are drawn. A commons needs a boundary (Ostrom) — but the boundary must fit real people (Scott), not the median professional.

1

Verification That Fits the Person

∗∗
Scott · Ostrom P1
Problem

A clear member/non-member boundary is required, but standard verification — address entry, license upload, utility bill — silently excludes the people furthest from the center: the PO-Box-only resident, the rancher without a license, the Spanish-speaking household.

Forces

The need for a legible, gameable-resistant boundary pulls against the diversity of how real Central Oregonians can prove where they live.

Solution

Offer multiple verification pathways (license, utility bill, voter registration, property record), human review for edge cases, PO-Box accommodation, and a Spanish-language flow. Verification must be legible to the person being verified, not just the administrator.

2

Verify, Then Forget

∗∗
Doctorow · counter-capture
Problem

Verification requires sensitive personal documents, but every document retained is a liability, a breach risk, and a thing a future actor could be tempted to monetize or a court could compel.

Forces

Trust requires proof of residency; non-extraction and member safety require holding as little as possible.

Solution

Delete verification PII immediately after use. Keep only the verified-status flag (and neighborhood, if the member set one). The platform that stores less is cheaper to trust and harder to capture.

3

The Participation Ladder

∗
Lave & Wenger
Problem

A newcomer should be able to take part without being either locked out at the door or handed full power on arrival.

Forces

Openness and welcome pull against resistance to capture by the newest or most-active members.

Solution

Make membership a ladder of legitimate peripheral participation: free (browse, see the community) → verified (RSVP, organize, vouch) → tenured (greater vote weight). Each rung is earned through participation, mirroring how competence and belonging actually grow.

4

Neighborhoods Drawn by Residents

∗
Jacobs · Scott
Problem

The platform needs to scope events and aid to a neighborhood — but a radius drawn on a map cuts off the neighbor across the street and never matches the lived shape of a community.

Forces

The system's need for tidy boundaries pulls against the mētis of where a neighborhood actually begins and ends.

Solution

Let residents define their own area — proximity to a point or proof of address — and let boundaries be adjusted by community process. Geography is data the community owns, not a circle an engineer guessed.

II

Voice & Governance

How the community decides, corrects itself, and keeps the door open to disagreement. Every governance pattern is a voice mechanism (Hirschman) — built so members speak rather than leave in silence.

5

Decide the Rules Before the Tool

∗∗
Postman · Meadows
Problem

If you build the enforcement infrastructure first, the tool quietly defines the institution — the technical system becomes the de-facto governance.

Forces

The urge to ship pulls against the need for the community's purposes to lead, not follow, the code.

Solution

Govern before you enforce. The governance design and the founding cohort's first real vote precede the features that enforce them. Pedagogy before platform; purpose before mechanism.

Leads to → 6 Tenure-Weighted Vote · 17 The Minimum Trustworthy Platform
6

The Tenure-Weighted Vote

∗∗
Ostrom P3
Problem

One-person-one-vote is captured by whoever is loudest and most-recently-active; pure seniority entrenches the old guard and silences newcomers.

Forces

New-member voice pulls against resistance to thin-participation and recency capture.

Solution

Combine a quorum with tenure weighting (1× to 3× by time in community — 1.0× in the first year, scaling to 3.0× past four). Newcomers vote from day one; long members carry the weight of having moved toward fuller membership. Neither lever alone is sufficient.

Leads to → 9 The Override · 3 The Participation Ladder
7

Action, Then Voice

∗∗
Hirschman · Ostrom P6
Problem

Moderation either blocks the action (too slow to keep people safe) or blocks the person's voice (they're removed and leave silently — the invisible, unteachable exit).

Forces

Safety and speed pull against fairness and the right to be heard.

Solution

Take the action; open the appeal immediately. Sanctions are graduated (warning → temp → extended → board review), never permanent without review. Voice follows action rather than being blocked by it.

Leads to → 9 The Override · 8 Vital Signs, Not Targets · 19 Human in the Loop
8

Vital Signs, Not Targets

∗∗
Meadows · O'Neil · Christian
Problem

You must read the platform's health, but any metric turned into a target is gamed and corrupted (Goodhart). The alignment problem is the same failure in sharper form: specify success in simplified, measurable terms and you optimize the proxy while losing the thing you actually wanted.

Forces

The need to monitor pulls against the way optimization quietly turns a measure into the mission — and turns a metric over human outcomes into a weapon.

Solution

Track leading indicators — exit-rate vs. appeal-rate, mod-queue volume, cross-neighborhood engagement — as diagnostics only. Never optimize them, never display them as member scores. High exit with low appeal is the alarm that voice is failing. The measure serves the judgment; it never replaces it.

Leads to → 15 One Flat Price, Never by Scope · 19 Human in the Loop
9

The Override

∗
Ostrom P6 · Hirschman
Problem

Even fair moderators err, and a single wrong decision left standing can calcify into distrust of the whole process.

Forces

Moderator authority (needed for order) pulls against the members' need to overrule it when it's wrong.

Solution

Let sustained complaints trigger a community override vote, and hold moderators to the same appeal system as members. Authority is real but never final against the community's voice.

III

Knowledge & Connection

The heart of the mission: putting people who hold local, practical knowledge in contact with where it's needed — without capturing, scoring, or formalizing it away (Polanyi, Scott). This is also where the line with Redmond Compass is drawn: participation, not discovery.

10

The Trust Graph, Not the Directory

∗∗
Polanyi · Scott · O'Neil · Illich
Problem

People need to find who can help — who fixes wells, who teaches canning, who'll mentor a welder. A ranked business directory turns that tacit, relational knowledge into a score, and duplicates the partner who already does commercial discovery.

Forces

Findability pulls against preserving knowledge that lives in relationships and can't be reduced to stars.

Solution

Build a trust graph of who-knows-how among verified neighbors — surfaced through vouching and relationship, never star-ranked. The question is "who can I trust to help," answered by people, not an algorithm. (Commercial listings stay with Compass.)

Leads to → 13 Mutual Aid With Dignity · 14 Match People, Not Credentials
11

Events as Participation, Not Listings

∗∗
Jacobs · Dewey · Illich
Problem

A "what's on this weekend" listings page is discovery — the partner's lane — and reading about an event builds nothing. Community is built by doing, not browsing.

Forces

The partner boundary and the community-building mission both pull away from passive listings.

Solution

Let verified neighbors create and join neighborhood-scale gatherings — repair cafés, work parties, potlucks, skill-shares, wildfire-prep. The act of hosting is the civic muscle the platform exists to build. "Eyes on the street" is participation density.

Leads to → 12 Just Enough Coordination · 4 Neighborhoods Drawn by Residents
12

Just Enough Coordination

∗
Illich · Norman
Problem

A gathering needs light coordination, but an open discussion thread becomes a place to argue and the single largest moderation burden — the very surface deferred for now.

Forces

Usefulness pulls toward more communication; the deferred-board risk pulls toward less.

Solution

Provide structured coordination that ends when the event does — RSVP, a who's-bringing-what list, carpool — and no free-text feed. Frictionless to contribute; nothing that becomes a standing forum.

Leads to → 11 Events as Participation · 22 Effort Where It Belongs
13

Mutual Aid With Dignity

∗
Care Collective · Illich
Problem

Asking for help can stigmatize, and aid boards ossify into stale, ignored lists.

Forces

The logic of care pulls toward visibility; dignity and freshness pull toward restraint.

Solution

Keep requests and offers low-ceremony, neighborhood-scoped, and expiring (≈30 days), with no ratings and no payments. Dignity in both asking and offering; people matching people, no institution between them.

Leads to → 10 The Trust Graph · 14 Match People, Not Credentials
14

Match People, Not Credentials

new∗
Illich (Deschooling) · Lave & Wenger · Dewey
Problem

A community holds enormous practical knowledge — how to can, weld, prune, fix a pump, navigate a county permit — but the usual way to "connect learners with teachers" is to build a credentialed service: courses, certifications, an institution in the middle that owns the relationship.

Forces

The wish to make knowledge findable and trustworthy pulls toward formal credentials and ratings; the mission to preserve tacit, apprenticed knowledge pulls against any intermediary that converts a commons into a service.

Solution

Build learning webs, not a school — directly match people who want to learn or trade a skill with neighbors who hold it, peer to peer, with no credential, no fee required, and no institution owning the exchange. Often the match simply becomes a skill-share gathering. The platform connects; it does not certify.

IV

Fair Access & Non-Extraction

How the platform funds itself without betraying the people it serves. Ownership and pricing are destiny (Baradaran); the refusal of extraction is structural (Doctorow, Illich).

15

One Flat Price, Never by Scope

∗∗
Baradaran · Doctorow
Problem

Charging more for a wider area and less for your own block feels fair — but breadth of access correlates with need: the cross-town commuter, the car-less resident, the newcomer, the job-seeker all need the whole city and are least able to pay more. It also installs a value-withhold the platform is later tempted to tighten, and splits a flat commons into classes.

Forces

Affordability and a flat commons pull against revenue and the ever-present enshittification temptation to gate.

Solution

One low flat price — the same for every member, the whole platform for everyone, never scaled to how much of the community you can reach; neighborhood focus is a free filter, never a paywall; and an optional contribution on top of dues — a few dollars more, or a custom amount — directed by the member toward running the commons or to where it's most needed. Everyone gets the same full platform for the same base price; what flexes is only what a member chooses to give beyond it.

Leads to → 16 The Tool You Can Leave · 8 Vital Signs, Not Targets
16

The Tool You Can Leave

∗∗
Illich · Doctorow (The Internet Con)
Problem

Platforms manufacture dependency and lock-in to hold users — the moment leaving costs you everything you made, the tool has begun to own you.

Forces

Stickiness and retention pull against conviviality and genuine member ownership.

Solution

Make it a tool a member can walk away from: data belongs to members and is exportable, nothing they made is held hostage, and there are no engagement traps or dark patterns. The right to exit — and, where possible, interoperability — is the mechanism that keeps the platform honest: a community that can leave is one the platform must keep serving.

25

Local Exchange, Value Stays Local

new∗
Baradaran · Ostrom · Scott · Doctorow
Problem

Verified neighbors want to trade goods, services, and skills with people they already trust — but the ordinary way to enable that is a marketplace, and a marketplace extracts: it takes a fee or a cut, sells ads and promoted slots, ranks listings, and turns trust into a star score that can be farmed and gamed.

Forces

The wish to make local trade easy and findable pulls toward marketplace mechanics — fees, featured listings, reputation scores; keeping value circulating inside the community pulls against every one of them, lest the platform become a rent-seeker or a perception game.

Solution

Build a neighborhood-scoped board where verified residents post offers and asks, browsed by proximity and time, exactly like events, in two deliberately separated layers. Committed (post-beta): the non-monetary core — a needs exchange ("I have firewood / I need firewood") and tradespeople trade-for-trade (barter of services, no money). Both share one listing primitive (offer/ask with a category), reused the way the event primitive was — not two parallel systems. The platform never sits in the transaction: no fee, no cut, no ads, no featured or ranked listings (chronological + neighborhood only, invariant 7). Trust is a fact, not a rank: a mutual, both-parties "this exchange happened, and I'd do it again," tied to one completed listing — never star ratings, scores, or leaderboards. Later, community-voted: a commercial (monetary) layer as an addition on top, gated behind a governance vote and not built until the cohort approves it — this deliberately separates civic exchange from commercial exchange and ensures the community decides when and whether to widen scope. Across both layers, disputes route through the existing appealable, separation-of-duties moderation, never mob-rating or guilt-by-association.

V

Building & Replication

How the thing gets built, kept honest in code, and carried to other communities. The structure is the lock — and the code is where the lock is cut.

17

The Minimum Trustworthy Platform

∗∗
Meadows
Problem

Shipping a broad feature set before trust exists is the most common way community platforms die — complexity arrives before the trust to carry it.

Forces

Ambition and the wish to feel complete pull against the slow fill-time of the trust stock.

Solution

Ship the smallest thing that is unmistakably Steppe — verification + one neighborhood-event primitive + transparency, with governance seeded by one real vote — then phase-gate everything else behind confirmed baseline health. Earn the right to add complexity.

18

The Constitution in the Data Layer

∗
Baradaran
Problem

Rules enforced only in the interface can be bypassed; if the UI is the only thing between a rule and its breach, the rule isn't real.

Forces

Developer convenience pulls toward client-side checks; structural integrity demands the lock sit deeper.

Solution

Enforce verification status, tenure weight, neighborhood scope, and moderation state at the row level (RLS), where the data lives — not patched on in the client. The governance model becomes structurally true, not merely displayed. The structure is the lock.

19

Human in the Loop on Consequence

∗∗
Weizenbaum
Problem

Automating consequential decisions removes the moral responsibility those decisions require — not because a machine can't perform them, but because no one then answers for them.

Forces

Scale and efficiency pull toward automation; judgment and accountability demand a person.

Solution

Automation may surface, sort, or flag — never decide. A person always makes the call on a ban, an appeal, or a governance outcome. Keep the decisions that carry moral weight with people who can be held responsible for them.

20

Built to Be Maintained

new∗
Eghbal (Working in Public) · Ostrom
Problem

Civic infrastructure has to outlast its founder, but the real constraint on software that lasts is not how much gets built — it's maintainer attention. A platform one person carries in their head dies when that person burns out or leaves; a founder-dependent project is not infrastructure.

Forces

The speed of a single builder pulls against the durability that only documentation, shared understanding, and a governed contributor base provide.

Solution

Treat maintenance as commons governance from the start: design for handoff, not heroics. Document decisions, keep the contribution surface small and legible, and grow a governed base of maintainers (the intern pipeline is the seed) under clear, Ostrom-style rules. Optimize for the attention the project will need in year five, not the features it can ship in month two.

21

Patterns, Offered Not Imposed

∗
Alexander
Problem

Carrying a working design to other towns — and one day to Warm Springs — risks imposing one community's answers on another, the very legibility failure the whole project refuses.

Forces

The value of transmissible, hard-won design pulls against local sovereignty and the mētis of each place.

Solution

Document every decision as problem → forces → solution → evidence — this document — and offer it as patterns a community can adopt, adapt, or refuse. Keep the reasoning attached to the answer so each place can re-decide in its own context. Replication without capture.

VI

Craft & Care

How the tool itself is made — the design judgment underneath every other pattern. A convivial, mētis-preserving platform is not only governed well; it is built well, for the whole range of bodies and minds that will use it.

22

Effort Where It Belongs

new∗∗
Kahneman · Norman
Problem

Bad design puts effort in the wrong place: it forces slow, deliberate attention (System 2) onto routine acts that should be effortless, and lets consequential acts slip by on fast, automatic habit (System 1). Both failures erode trust — one exhausts people, the other lets them act without weighing what they're doing.

Forces

The drive to make everything frictionless pulls against the fact that some decisions should be slow, felt, and deliberate.

Solution

Match the cognitive mode to the stakes. Make routine acts effortless — one-tap RSVP, obvious affordances, no manual (System 1). Make consequential acts deliberate — the 30-day deliberation on immutable principles, the read-and-confirm on terms, a real pause before an irreversible vote (System 2). Friction is not a flaw to be removed; it is a tool to be placed exactly where judgment is required.

Leads to → 12 Just Enough Coordination · 23 Deep Modules, Narrow Interfaces
23

Deep Modules, Narrow Interfaces

new∗∗
Ousterhout
Problem

Complexity is the thing that kills software slowly: every shallow component with a wide, leaky interface spreads its complexity across everything that touches it, until no one can change one part without breaking three others. For a platform meant to last and to be handed off, accumulated complexity is an existential risk, not a cosmetic one.

Forces

The pressure to ship the next feature fast pulls against the discipline that keeps the system comprehensible enough to keep changing safely.

Solution

Build deep modules behind narrow interfaces — each component does one substantial job and hides how. Pull complexity downward (the platform absorbs hard work so the member and the next developer don't), design it twice before committing, and define errors out of existence rather than handling them everywhere. Simplicity at the seams is what makes patterns 17 and 20 — minimal, maintainable — achievable in practice.

24

Built for Every Body

new∗∗
Shew (Against Technoableism) · Norman
Problem

Technology built for an imagined "normal" user excludes disabled residents — and the common "fix" (a bolted-on accessibility mode, a tool that frames disabled people as problems to be solved) is its own harm: techno-ableism. A civic commons that doesn't work for everyone is not a commons.

Forces

The convenience of designing for a single default user pulls against the truth that disabled residents are full members with their own expertise about what they need.

Solution

Design with disabled residents, not for an imagined norm. Accessibility is a first-class requirement of the core build — screen-reader support, keyboard paths, contrast, captions, plain language, low-bandwidth tolerance — never a later mode. This is Pattern 1 extended to the body: verification, voice, and participation must fit the whole range of people, or the boundary quietly excludes again.

This is v2 — and a pattern language is never finished

Alexander's languages grew and were corrected by the people who built with them. So will this one. Patterns earn their confidence markers in the field: the fieldwork, the closed beta, and the first real conflicts will promote some to ∗∗, demote others, and reveal patterns not yet written.

It helps to remember what these patterns are reaching back toward. A community that owns its own infrastructure, governs a commons, and preserves its own practical knowledge is not a radical experiment — across millennia it is closer to the human norm, and the extractive platform monoculture is the recent anomaly. The work is less invention than recovery.

The discipline is to keep adding patterns the way the community teaches them — with the forces and the local evidence intact — so that what travels to the next town is not a template but a way of reasoning a sovereign community can make its own.
