## TL;DR
- **The most important finding: Connect Central Oregon does NOT operate a formal fiscal-sponsorship program** — "fiscal sponsorship" appears only as a single unsupported word in its services menu on a partly unfinished website, with no application, fee, agreement, or eligibility criteria. Treat CCO as a *programmatic* partner and referral node, not a sponsor. (CCO had previously been assumed to be the first call.)
- **At the time of this research, no Central Oregon (and effectively no Oregon-statewide) 501(c)(3) had been identified running a civic-tech-suitable fiscal-sponsorship program** *(superseded — see the status update above)*. Because Steppe's first priority is faster grant access, the recommendation was to bridge through **Aspiration** — a national civic-tech Model C sponsor whose own site states "Fiscal sponsorship fees range from 5%-15% of revenues," charges a "$150" non-refundable application fee, and runs sponsorships that "generally… last 1-2 years" — while building local rootedness through programmatic partners and letters of support. Leading funders (OCF, Roundhouse, Ford Family) explicitly accept out-of-state fiscal sponsors.
- **Approach these five programmatic partners first, in this order:** (1) Deschutes Public Library / DPL Foundation, (2) Latino Community Association, (3) the Oregon Broadband Equity Coalition (free to join now), (4) Citizens4Community (Sisters), and (5) Thrive Central Oregon — chosen because they maximize both grant-access credibility and local embeddedness without creating dependency or governance conflicts.

## 1) WHAT IS POSSIBLE — Taxonomy of Relationships Available to Steppe Now

| Relationship type | What it does for Steppe | What it cannot do | Primarily serves |
|---|---|---|---|
| **(a) Fiscal sponsorship — Model C (pre-approved grant)** | Lets Steppe receive tax-deductible restricted launch grants in 2026 *through* a 501(c)(3) sponsor while keeping its own board, identity, and IP; sponsor re-grants funds to Steppe and provides financial oversight. Temporary bridge that dissolves on Steppe's own IRS determination. | Does not give Steppe its own exempt status; does not let the sponsor control governance if structured correctly; not a permanent home. | **Grant access** (primary) |
| **(a-alt) Fiscal sponsorship — Model A (comprehensive)** | Sponsor legally houses the project; staff become sponsor employees; deepest back-office support (HR, payroll, audit). | Conflicts with Steppe's member-controlled governance and "reversible decisions / member-owned data" commitments — the project legally *belongs* to the sponsor under Model A. Use only as a last resort. | Grant access (but governance risk) |
| **(b) Ongoing programmatic partnership (MOU / co-delivery / referral / joint programming)** | Co-deliver community programming, cross-refer users, share outreach, embed Steppe in trusted local institutions; generates letters of support that strengthen grant applications. | Does not move money to Steppe directly; not a legal funding pathway. | **Rootedness** (primary), with indirect grant-access lift |
| **(c) Coalition / network membership** | Visibility, shared learning, policy alignment, and funder signal (e.g., OBEC ties Steppe to the state Digital Equity Plan and BEAD/Digital Equity Act funding language). | Confers no money or exempt status; influence is diffuse. | Rootedness + funding *signal* |
| **(d) Named collaborator / sub-grantee / pass-through on a partner's grant** | A partner with 501(c)(3) status applies and names Steppe as a sub-recipient or pass-through, routing restricted funds to Steppe's work before its own determination. | Makes Steppe dependent on the partner's priorities and timeline; can blur governance and trigger the >30%-single-funder guardrail if one partner dominates. | **Grant access**, but watch dependency |

**Why Oregon-local is preferred but not available:** Rural Oregon funders value place-based legitimacy, and a Bend/Deschutes sponsor would read as more "of the community." But none exists for civic tech, and the leading funders explicitly accept out-of-state sponsors — OCF instructs that fiscal-sponsor DOJ registration "applies to your fiscal sponsor, regardless of whether the sponsor is in-state or out-of-state," and Roundhouse and Ford Family both fund fiscally-sponsored projects "actively in pursuit of 501(c)(3) status." The credibility gap is therefore best closed with *local programmatic partners and letters of support*, not the sponsor's address.

## 4) STRENGTHS & WEAKNESSES — General to Steppe's Mission

**General strengths a partnership + fiscal-sponsor strategy brings:**
- **Faster grant access (priority #1):** A Model C sponsor unlocks 2026 restricted grants (OCF Fall cycle, Roundhouse, Ford Family, Bend Foundation) months before Steppe's own determination. Partners' letters of support and named-collaborator roles materially raise scores — OCF advises applicants to "explicitly demonstrate how the communities you serve provide meaningful input."
- **Local rootedness & credibility (priority #2):** Libraries, LCA, and C4C confer trust Steppe cannot manufacture alone — directly serving the **Ostrom** principle that durable commons are governed by, and recognized within, the community that uses them.
- **Embeddedness & reach:** LCA and the tri-county libraries put Steppe in all six target towns and in Spanish, satisfying the mandatory-local-verification and Spanish-localization goals.
- **Shared learning & funder signal:** OBEC ties Steppe to state Digital Equity funding logic at zero cost and zero lock-in.

**General weaknesses / risks to guard against, tied to Steppe's named principles:**
- **Dependency / lock-in (anti-enshittification, Doctorow; "no dependency moats"):** A comprehensive Model A sponsor or a single dominant pass-through partner would create exactly the lock-in Steppe exists to prevent. Mitigate by using Model C (Steppe keeps its board and IP) and a clean spin-out clause.
- **Funder concentration >30%:** A large pass-through grant from one partner could breach the 30% rule. Track sponsor-routed revenue against the cap from day one.
- **Dilution of member governance (Baradaran ownership; member-owned data):** Any partner expecting editorial control, data access, or branding rights conflicts with member control. State data ownership and reversibility in every MOU.
- **Reputational entanglement (Hirschman exit/voice/loyalty):** Tying Steppe to a for-profit-linked entity (Source Weekly/Lay It Out) or a politically exposed partner could compromise neutrality; preserve "exit" by keeping all relationships terminable and non-exclusive.
- **Capacity strain (Meadows leverage points):** A tiny startup courting many partners can collapse under coordination load. Sequence deliberately (below); the highest-leverage intervention is one sponsor + five partners, not twenty.

- **Researched first choice: Aspiration (Model C).** It is the only realistic civic-tech-fluent 501(c)(3) that (i) accepts community-infrastructure/data projects, (ii) keeps Steppe's board, IP, and member governance intact (its model "is typically that of a grantor (Aspiration) and grantee (Project) relationship, so while the project is being sponsored, the project remains a separate entity with its own Board of Directors"), (iii) is designed as a 1–2 year temporary bridge with a clean spin-out, and (iv) charges a defensible 5–15%. **The ask:** "Will Aspiration serve as Steppe's Model C fiscal sponsor to receive restricted 2026 launch grants, with a spin-out on our IRS determination?"
