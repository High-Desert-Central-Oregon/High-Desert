Orientation

This document moves Steppe from plan to build. The business plan establishes why the structure holds. This establishes what to construct first, how it should feel, and how it should be engineered — with each decision traceable to the canon. The emphasis here is the six tool-design books now carried in both the reading list and Business Plan v11 (Polanyi, Schön, Illich, Weizenbaum, Norman, Alexander) — the ones that answer the build question directly, and so are load-bearing here. Reading List v11 sharpens the build craft further: Ousterhout on containing complexity, Eghbal on maintenance as commons governance, and Shew on accessibility as design with disabled residents — all folded into the practices below. Two scope decisions from v11 also shape this build: launch is Redmond-first, and access is one flat price with an optional voluntary contribution — never gated by geographic scope.

The organizing claim: Steppe is a convivial tool that preserves mētis. In Illich's exact sense, it amplifies the community's own capacity without requiring the community to serve the platform. In Scott's and Polanyi's sense, it puts people who hold local, tacit, practical knowledge in contact with situations where that knowledge operates — rather than trying to capture, score, or formalize it away. Everything below is an instance of that one idea.

01

The Principle Stack

Six operative layers. Each names the thinkers, the single claim that matters for building, and what it dictates in the product. The plan covered the first three. Layers four through six are largely new and come straight from the v11 reading list.

Structural — why it survives

Doctorow · Baradaran · Wu · Lanier

Extraction is structural, not moral. A second customer (advertisers) inevitably wins against the first (users); private interests capture public structures one defensible step at a time; attention has been colonized by the ad model for 150 years.

Dictates: No advertising, ever. Members are the only customer. Data belongs to members. Counter-capture is encoded, not promised — immutable principles changeable only by 75% supermajority + 30-day deliberation.

Systems — how it behaves

Meadows

Trust is the master stock: long fill time, fast drain time. System behavior comes from loop structure, not feature lists. The paradigm is the highest leverage point.

Dictates: Build the minimum trustworthy platform first and earn the right to add complexity. Read health through leading indicators, never optimize them. Every feature is a deposit to or withdrawal from the trust stock.

Commons — how it's governed

Ostrom · Hirschman

Shared resources can be governed durably without privatization or top-down control — if eight design principles hold. When institutions decline, silence is not consent; it is exit produced by costly voice.

Dictates: Clear boundaries (verification), collective choice (tenure-weighted voting), graduated sanctions, monitoring (transparency reports). Every governance feature is a voice mechanism: ban-then-appeal, public log, community override.

Knowledge — what it preserves new emphasis

Scott · Polanyi · Schön · Jacobs

"We know more than we can tell." Institutions simplify to govern, and simplification destroys the tacit, local knowledge that made things work. You cannot fully capture mētis; you can only build conditions for it to operate.

Dictates: Verification legible to the verified, not just the admin. Designs vary by subregion rather than templating from Bend. Features surface who knows how instead of ranking and scoring it. The design question is never "how do we capture local knowledge?" but "how do we connect people who hold it to where it's needed?"

Tool — how it's built new

Illich · Weizenbaum · Norman · Alexander · Ousterhout

Convivial tools amplify capability without creating dependency; industrial tools make users serve the tool. Some decisions must stay with humans not because machines can't perform them but because they require moral responsibility. Good design needs no manual. Mētis can be made transmissible — as patterns — without becoming rigid theory. And complexity is the slow killer: deep modules behind narrow interfaces are how it's contained.

Dictates: Consequential decisions (moderation outcomes, governance) stay with people; automation assists, never decides. Affordances over instructions. Contain complexity behind deep modules and narrow interfaces, so the platform stays comprehensible enough to keep changing and to hand off. Document each design choice as problem → forces → solution → evidence, so it travels to other towns without being imposed.

Learning — what it teaches new

Dewey · Lave & Wenger · Illich · Kahneman · Postman

Democracy is associated living — learned by practicing, not reading. Competence transmits through legitimate peripheral participation in communities of practice. Tools quietly colonize the institutions that adopt them unless purpose precedes the technical system.

Dictates: Onboarding is a participation ladder (browse → contribute → organize → govern), with tenure weighting reflecting the climb. Mutual aid, skill-shares and the trades layer are learning webs — people matching people, no credentialing institution between them. Governance is decided before the infrastructure to enforce it is built.

02

Scope & the Compass Boundary

Steppe and Redmond Compass are complementary, not competing. Compass is the public discovery guide — anyone can read it. Steppe is the verified-resident community-building layer — you participate in it. The line is discovery vs. participation. Steppe does not rebuild what Compass already does well; it builds the thing that requires verified identity and trust. Launch is Redmond-first — one community served completely, matching Compass's footprint — then the same pattern branches to the other Central Oregon communities.

Redmond Compass Partner · owns

  • Small-business directory & owner dashboards
  • Public event listings (discover what's happening)
  • Local news
  • Essential community resources

Steppe Owns

  • Verified residency — the trust substrate
  • Neighbor-organized, neighborhood-scale events (participation, not listing)
  • Mutual aid, skills & trades trust, learning webs
  • Democratic governance, moderation, transparency

Deferred — logged Later

  • Digital community board (regional discussion feed)
  • Reason: highest-conflict / highest-moderation-risk surface; phase-gating says don't open it until the trust stock is high (Meadows). Discussion-adjacent needs are partly met by Compass's community page.
  • Re-open later by community vote once verification, events, aid and governance are proven.

Do not duplicate Avoid

  • A second business directory (Compass has it)
  • A public "what's on this weekend" listings page
  • A local news desk
  • Later: a thin optional bridge so HD neighborhood events can surface to Compass's public guide — logged, not built now.
03

Feature Architecture

Three tiers. The trust substrate is what makes Steppe categorically different from Compass and from social media — everything sits on it. Neighborhood events are the headline build. Community-building components are the relationship and capacity layer. Each card carries its principle tags, a design note, and a build note.

Tier A — Trust Substrate

launch-critical · the loop everything depends on
A1

Residency verification

Multiple pathways (driver's license, utility bill, voter registration, property record), human review for edge cases, PO Box accommodation for Warm Springs, Spanish-language flow in Madras/Jefferson County.

Scott · legibilityOstrom P1 · boundariesprivacy-by-architecture
Design

Legible to the person being verified, not just the administrator. The rancher with a PO Box and the teen without a license both succeed.

Build

Verify-then-forget: PII deleted post-verification. Verification status becomes a row-level flag that gates everything downstream.

A2

Tenure-weighted governance & voting

Community votes on platform policy. Quorum requirements + tenure weighting (1×–3×) prevent capture by the most-active or most-recent users. Immutable principles need 75% + 30-day deliberation.

Ostrom P3 · collective choiceLave & Wenger · communities of practiceBaradaran · counter-capture
Design

Tenure weight reflects the participation ladder — longer members have moved toward fuller membership. Seed it small: the founding cohort's first vote is a real, low-stakes decision.

Build

Thresholds and tenure brackets live in config + RLS, versioned. Every vote writes to an immutable audit log.

A3

Moderation — graduated & appealable

Warning → temp ban → extended ban → board review. No permanent bans without review. Ban-then-appeal. Sustained complaints against a moderator decision trigger a community override vote.

Ostrom P5/6 · sanctions + conflictHirschman · voiceWeizenbaum · human-in-loop
Design

Action does not block voice — the appeal opens immediately. Calibrate to Central Oregon's actual high-heat topics, catalogued in fieldwork, not generic templates.

Build

Outcomes are never auto-decided. The model may surface, sort, or flag; a human always decides. Moderators are subject to the same appeal path.

A4

Transparency & platform-health dashboard

Quarterly public reports (revenue, expenses, founder salary, moderation actions). Internal dashboard of leading indicators: posting frequency, cross-regional engagement, mod-queue volume, exit-rate vs. appeal-rate.

Ostrom P4 · monitoringMeadows · leading indicatorsO'Neil · anti-WMD
Design

The exit-vs-appeal ratio is the early-warning gauge: high exit + low appeal means voice is failing. Watch it like a vital sign.

Build

Indicators are diagnostics, never targets. A metric optimized becomes a weapon (Goodhart / O'Neil). No engagement-maximizing logic anywhere.

A5

Plain-language terms & read-confirm

Terms and privacy written to be understood, with an interactive read-confirm flow and archived version history.

Norman · affordanceKahneman · cognitive loadScott · legible to the user
Design

If a member can't explain the rule back, the rule isn't written yet. Spanish parity, not afterthought.

Build

Versioned documents; consent records tied to the version a member actually accepted.

Tier B — Neighborhood-Level Events

the headline · participation, not listings
B1

Neighbor-organized events

Any verified resident can create a neighborhood-scale gathering — block party, repair café, trail or irrigation work party, potluck, skill-share, wildfire-prep meet. This is the creation and joining of events, which is what separates it from Compass's discovery listings.

Jacobs · eyes on the streetDewey · associated livingIllich · convivial
Design

Organizers are verified neighbors, never anonymous. The act of hosting is the civic muscle the platform exists to build.

Build

Events inherit the A1–A3 trust + moderation layer for free. One clean primitive (create / RSVP / show up) before anything fancy.

B2

Neighborhood geography

Events scope to neighborhood, not just region — the "neighborhood-level" requirement. Region → neighborhood granularity, with each community defining its own shape. Scope is a free focus filter — never a paywall. A member can narrow to their block or widen to all of Redmond at no cost; breadth is never withheld.

Jacobs · participation densityOstrom P2 · local conditionsScott · don't template from BendBaradaran · access not gated by scope
Design

Let neighborhoods be drawn by residents, not imposed by a tidy grid. Sisters is not Bend is not Madras. Narrowing is a default/preference, not a purchased tier.

Build

Geography is data, editable by community process — not hard-coded boundaries an engineer guessed. The scope filter lives in the UI; it must never become a billing gate.

B3

Light coordination — not a chat board

RSVP, a "who's bringing what" list, simple carpool/ride coordination. Just enough to make a gathering happen. Deliberately not open-ended discussion — that's the deferred board.

Illich · amplify, don't trapNorman · no manual neededKahneman · one-tap
Design

Frictionless to contribute; nothing that becomes a place to argue. Coordination ends when the event does.

Build

Structured fields, not a free-text feed. Keeps moderation surface tiny and intentional.

B4

Standing & recurring gatherings

Repair cafés, weekly walks, monthly skill-shares — events that repeat and accrete a regular group.

Lave & Wenger · communities of practicebrown · speed of trust
Design

Recurrence is how a community of practice forms. The platform should make returning easier than starting over.

Build

Recurring series as first-class objects; a returning attendee is a soft signal of belonging, never a public score.

Tier C — Community-Building Components

relationships & mutual capacity
C1

Mutual aid board

Requests and offers within a neighborhood — a ride, a tool, help after a storm, an extra freezer during wildfire season. Items expire (≈30 days) so the board stays current.

Care Manifesto · care as logicIllich · learning webbrown · critical connections
Design

People matching people, no institution between them. Low ceremony; dignity in both asking and offering.

Build

Expiry by default. Neighborhood-scoped via RLS. No payments, no ratings.

C2

Skills & trades trust layer

Not a business directory (Compass owns that). This is the trust graph of practical know-how among verified neighbors — who can fix a fence, who knows wells, who teaches canning, who'll mentor a welder. Mētis made findable, without a credentialing institution.

Scott / Polanyi · tacit knowledgeLave & WengerO'Neil · no scoringIllich · deschooling
Design

Vouching through verified relationships, not star ratings. The question is "who can I trust to help," answered by people, not an algorithm. This is the sharpest line away from Compass and the clearest mētis application.

Build

A trust/skill graph, not a ranked list. Any ranking would be a WMD here — default to relationship and proximity. No payments on-platform.

C3

Skill exchange & learning webs

Non-monetary teach/learn and barter — Spanish lessons for fly-tying, fence repair for childcare. The connective tissue between events (B), the trades trust graph (C2), and aid (C1).

Illich · convivialityDewey · learning by doingCare Manifesto
Design

Frame as reciprocity and learning, not a marketplace. Often the right surface is simply a skill-share event (B4).

Build

Reuse the events + trades primitives rather than building a fourth silo. Compose, don't multiply.

C4

Onboarding as a participation ladder

The free → verified → tenured arc made intentional. Newcomers begin at the periphery (browse public events, see the community) and move toward fuller participation (RSVP, organize, vouch, govern) as competence and identity develop.

Lave & Wenger · legitimate peripheral participationHirschman · voice over time
Design

The free/paid tier split is "accidentally correct" in Lave & Wenger's terms — this makes it deliberate. Each rung should feel earned, not gated.

Build

Capabilities unlock by verification + tenure state, enforced in RLS, mirrored honestly in the UI.

C5

Community resilience alerts

Opt-in wildfire / weather / infrastructure alerts with redundant delivery. High value given Central Oregon's wildfire stress; reads as neighbors looking out for neighbors, not a feed.

civic infrastructureOstrom P2 · local conditions
Design

Trusted, sparse, never noisy. Earns its place precisely by being rare.

Build

Phase it after the substrate proves out; push + SMS redundancy. Architecture logged now, built later.

04

Design Practice

How the surfaces should feel. The through-line: frictionless to contribute, deliberate where it counts. Most platforms invert this — effortless outrage, effortful exit. Steppe reverses it.

Norman

Affordances over instructions

Every surface — the RSVP, the appeal form, the transparency report — should be usable without a manual. If it needs explaining, it needs redesigning.

Do — make the obvious action the easy action; design invites use.
Don't — ship a feature that requires a help doc to operate.
Kahneman

Friction by design

Reserve System-2 effort for high-stakes moments and remove it everywhere else. Joining an event is one tap (System 1). Changing an immutable principle is a 30-day deliberation (System 2 on purpose).

Do — one-tap to help, RSVP, vouch.
Don't — add friction to participation or remove it from consequence.
Illich · Doctorow

Pass the conviviality test

Each feature must amplify the community's capability without creating dependency on the platform. A member can always leave with their data and lose nothing they made. The right to exit — and, where feasible, interoperability — is the mechanism that keeps the platform honest: a community that can walk away is one the platform must keep serving.

Do — exportable data; an unobstructed exit; tools that work even if HD vanished.
Don't — lock-in, dark patterns, or engagement traps.
Baradaran · Doctorow

Fair access — price by means, not scope

Affordability is a design problem, not a billing afterthought. One low flat price — the same for everyone, the whole platform for all — with an optional contribution on top of dues that the member directs toward running the commons or to where it's most needed. Never price by how much of the community a member is allowed to see — breadth of access correlates with need, so scope-pricing is regressive and installs a value-withhold the platform is later tempted to tighten.

Do — flex price to means; make neighborhood focus a free filter; let everyone have the whole platform.
Don't — gate access by geography, feature-tier the commons, or raise price without a community vote.
Scott · Hirschman

Legible to the user · voice-first

Verification, moderation and governance are explained in the member's terms, not the admin's. Any action affecting a member ships with a response path in the same view.

Do — show the rule, the reason, and the appeal together.
Don't — take an action a member can't see, understand, or contest.
Alexander

Write the pattern language

Document each design choice as problem → forces → solution → evidence. This is now an explicit companion artifact — A Pattern Language for Civic Infrastructure (v2) — the thing that lets the design travel to other towns, and eventually to Warm Springs, without being imposed. Replication without capture.

Do — record the forces and the local evidence behind each choice.
Don't — ship a decision as a rule with no rationale attached.
Shew · Norman

Built for every body — accessible by default

Design with disabled residents, not for an imagined norm — and never as a bolted-on "accessibility mode." Screen-reader support, keyboard paths, contrast, captions, and plain language are first-class requirements of the core build. This is "verification that fits the person" extended to the body: if the platform doesn't work for everyone, the boundary quietly excludes again.

Do — ship semantic markup, keyboard navigation, and real contrast from the first screen; ask disabled members what they need.
Don't — treat accessibility as a later pass, or frame disabled people as problems for tech to "fix."
accessibility · i18n

Spanish-first, low-bandwidth, mobile-first

Madras parity is a Day-1 design constraint, not a translation pass. Rural connectivity and phones-as-primary-device shape every layout.

Do — design Spanish and English together; test on a slow phone.
Don't — assume Bend broadband and a desktop.
05

Software Development Practice

How it's engineered. The governing idea: the constitution should be true in the data layer, not just the UI. Structure is the lock (Baradaran); code is where the lock is cut.

Meadows

Minimum trustworthy platform, then earn complexity

Ship the smallest thing that earns trust; phase-gate everything after. No new surface opens until baseline health is confirmed. Feature creep before trust is the documented common failure.

Ousterhout

Deep modules, narrow interfaces — contain complexity

Complexity is what kills software slowly. Build deep modules behind narrow interfaces — each does one substantial job and hides how. Pull complexity downward (the platform absorbs hard work so the member and the next developer don't), design it twice before committing, and define errors out of existence rather than handling them everywhere. Simplicity at the seams is what keeps the minimum platform minimal and the codebase handoff-ready.

Supabase · Postgres

Row-Level Security as the constitution in code

Verification status, tenure weight, neighborhood/region scope, and moderation state are enforced at the row level — not patched on in the client. The governance model is structurally true where the data lives, so the UI can't be the only thing standing between a rule and its breach.

Baradaran · Doctorow

Scope is a filter, affordability is the billing layer — keep them apart

Neighborhood scope is a focus filter enforced in the UI and backed by RLS — it shapes what a member sees, never what they're allowed to pay for. Affordability lives entirely at the billing layer (one flat price, with optional contributions). The two must never be coupled in code: no entitlement check that ties geographic breadth to subscription tier. Every verified member's row grants the whole platform.

immutable principle

Reversibility & the audit log

Every major decision is reversible by community vote, so the system must support it: version T&C, governance config, moderation policy, neighborhood maps. Soft-delete, never hard-erase community record. Votes and moderation actions write to an append-only log.

Doctorow · counter-capture

Privacy by architecture — verify then forget

Collect the minimum; delete verification PII after use. The platform that stores less is cheaper to trust and harder to capture or subpoena. Data belongs to members and is exportable on request.

O'Neil · Christian

Anti-WMD engineering

No opaque scoring, ranking, or recommendation that severs the feedback loop from the people it affects. Default to chronological and proximity. If anything ever ranks, the affected members must see and shape the logic — and it never becomes an optimization target. The alignment problem is the warning underneath this: specify success in simplified, measurable terms and you optimize the proxy while losing what you actually wanted. Keep the measurable serving human judgment, never substituting for it.

Weizenbaum · Illich

Convivial architecture · human-in-the-loop

Keep consequential decisions with people. Automation may surface, sort, or flag; it never decides a ban, an appeal, or a governance outcome. No engagement-maximizing mechanics in the codebase, full stop.

Scott · Lave & Wenger

A legible codebase for the intern pipeline

The platform is built by the community it serves. Readable, documented, conventionally-structured code is legitimate peripheral participation for Tech-track interns and community devs — they start at the periphery and move inward. Resilience requires that no single person (the founder) is the only one who understands it.

Eghbal · Ostrom

Built to be maintained — design for handoff, not heroics

Civic infrastructure must outlast its founder, and the real constraint on software that lasts is maintainer attention, not how much gets built. Treat maintenance as commons governance: document decisions, keep the contribution surface small and legible, and grow a governed base of maintainers (the intern pipeline is the seed) under clear, Ostrom-style rules. Optimize for the attention the project will need in year five, not the features it can ship in month two.

The First Build Slice

Given all of the above, here is the minimum trustworthy platform — the smallest thing that is unmistakably Steppe and earns the right to grow. It is the Build loop made concrete: min. trustworthy platform → earn trust → earn complexity.

Verification + one event primitive + transparency, with governance seeded

Not a community board. Not a marketplace. Not fifteen features. The substrate that makes trust possible, the single most community-building surface (neighbors making real gatherings happen), and the visible honesty that proves the model — with the founding cohort casting one real governance vote so the democracy is true from the first week.

Residency verification (A1) — multi-pathway, PO Box + Spanish flows, verify-then-forget. The boundary that separates members from everyone, enforced in RLS.
Neighbor-organized events, one clean primitive (B1 + B3) — create, RSVP, light coordination, scoped to neighborhood (B2). No open discussion surface.
Graduated, appealable moderation (A3) — tiny by design because the only surface is structured events. Human decides; appeal opens immediately.
Transparency + health vitals (A4) — public report scheduled from Day 30; exit-vs-appeal ratio watched internally as the early-warning gauge.
Governance seeded (A2) — founding cohort's first real vote (a naming or norm decision) proves the loop works before it carries weight.
GATE → do not widen until baseline health holds: two trained mods · Spanish complete · payments tested · RLS reviewed. Mutual aid (C1) and the trades trust layer (C2) are the next slice, not this one.
