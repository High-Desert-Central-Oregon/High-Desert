## 01 — The Case in One Paragraph

***Steppe is community-owned digital infrastructure for the towns
of Central Oregon — a verified, ad-free, member-governed platform where
neighbors share events, needs, and decisions. It launches in Redmond in
late 2026, built and governed to stay member-owned, dues-funded, and
accountable to the people it serves. Dues of $4 a month fund its core
operations forever; grant support funds what dues alone cannot cover:
bilingual access, accessibility, youth workforce development,
and expansion to the rural towns that need it most.***

## 02 — The Need

Central Oregon’s communities have no shared information infrastructure
they own or trust. The regional newspaper has retrenched. National
platforms fill the vacuum badly: feeds engineered for outrage, no
connection to place, no accountability to residents, and business models
that treat communities as inventory. Word of mouth remains the most
trusted local channel — and it cannot scale.

The cost lands hardest outside Bend. Redmond, Sisters, La Pine, Madras,
Prineville, Terrebonne, and Culver — communities of trades, agriculture,
and working families — have the least local coverage and the least
digital infrastructure built for them. Roughly one in three Madras
residents speaks Spanish at home, and almost nothing in the region’s
civic information layer does. When a wildfire approaches, when a school
board decides, when a neighbor needs firewood or has it to give, there
is no trusted, verified place where that information moves.

This is not a content problem; it is an ownership problem. Every
previous platform serving the region answers to advertisers or distant
shareholders. The infrastructure failed because of who owned it — so the
fix has to start there.

## 03 — The Solution

Steppe is a member-governed Oregon nonprofit operating a community
platform that residents verify into, pay $4 a month for, and govern by
vote. The structure is the innovation:

  - **Owned by no one, governed by members** — an Oregon public benefit
    nonprofit with statutory voting members; no shares, no exit, a
    dissolution clause routing assets to aligned local charities. The
    commitments — no advertising ever, member-owned data, community
    control — are written into the articles of incorporation

  - **Verified and local** — membership requires proof of residency
    through multiple pathways with human review; the platform deletes
    verification documents the moment a decision is made

  - **Calm to use** — feeds ordered chronologically by time and
    proximity, so the platform surfaces what is recent and nearby

  - **Democratic in fact** — secret-ballot member votes with tenure
    weighting and quorum rules, enforced in the database itself;
    quarterly public transparency reports; moderation with due process
    and appeal

  - **Already built** — the platform is complete and security-audited as
    of June 2026 — verification, neighborhood-scoped events, governance
    voting, moderation, Spanish localization, accessibility, and mobile
    — with 35 Redmond neighborhoods mapped. Grant support does not
    fund a hope; it finishes and extends a working system

The design draws on established scholarship in commons governance,
systems thinking, and political economy — the platform’s governance is
audited against the eight design principles that distinguish community
resources that endure from those that collapse, and its structure is
built against the documented decay pattern of advertising-funded
platforms. A full sources statement is available on request.

## 04 — Who We Serve

  - **Now** — Redmond, Oregon (\~36,000 residents, 35 mapped
    neighborhoods) — launch late 2026, with Redmond Compass as community
    partner and two of three founding board members rooted in Redmond

  - **Next** — the rural towns of Central Oregon — Sisters, La Pine,
    Madras, Prineville, Terrebonne, Culver — joining by petition and
    community vote, with full Spanish-language access

  - **Always** — capped at twelve communities by charter. Steppe is
    built to serve a place deeply, not to scale; the cap is a structural
    promise that growth will never outrun governance

  - **Beneath the platform** — Steppe Connect extends the mission
    to access itself: rural connectivity pilots with community and
    cooperative partners, makerspace-built public access devices, and —
    on the long horizon, at the Tribes’ invitation — a tribal
    digital-sovereignty program built on complete tribal ownership of
    platform, infrastructure, data, and governance

## 06 — Outcomes & How We Measure

  - **Membership & reach** — verified members (targets: 100 by end of
    2026; 200 — roughly 0.5% of Redmond — by mid-2027); neighborhoods
    active; bilingual usage rates

  - **Civic participation** — governance votes held and turnout;
    community proposals submitted; events posted and attended

  - **Trust & fairness** — appeal rate vs. quiet-exit rate (our earliest
    health signal); moderation actions and outcomes published quarterly;
    transparency reports delivered on schedule

  - **Rural & bilingual access** — listening sessions completed per
    town; Spanish-language adoption in Madras-area outreach;
    verification accommodation cases resolved (PO box, non-standard
    documents)

  - **Youth workforce (year two)** — interns hired and paid; showcase
    delivered; post-internship placement

Every measure above is already instrumented in the platform’s health
dashboard or transparency-report pipeline — reporting to funders uses
the same numbers the public sees, which is the point.

## 08 — Financial Model & Sustainability

Steppe is built so that no funder — public or private — ever
becomes a dependency. Member dues of $4 a month fund core operations
permanently: at 500 members, operations are covered; at 1,000,
comfortably. Grant funding is additive by rule — it builds capacity and
expansion, never keeps the lights on. No single funder may exceed 30% of
annual revenue, no funding is accepted whose conditions touch the
founding commitments, and every grant and its conditions are published
in the quarterly transparency report. Surplus beyond operations flows to
local charities chosen each year by ranked-choice member vote. Funders
are not asked to sustain Steppe; they are asked to extend what a
self-sustaining commons can reach.
