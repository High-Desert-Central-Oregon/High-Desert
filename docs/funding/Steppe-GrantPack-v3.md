### Project Title

Civic Infrastructure for Rural Central Oregon: A Community-Owned
Platform, From Redmond Outward

### Need Statement

Central Oregon’s rural towns are losing the connective tissue that
healthy communities run on. Local news has retrenched; the national
platforms that filled the vacuum are engineered for outrage, owned
elsewhere, and accountable to no one here. The cost lands hardest in the
working communities — Redmond, Sisters, La Pine, Madras, Prineville,
Terrebonne, Culver — where coverage is thinnest, where a third of Madras
residents speak Spanish at home and the civic information layer doesn’t,
and where wildfire seasons make trustworthy local information a safety
issue, not a convenience. The deeper problem is ownership: every
platform serving the region extracts from it. Information infrastructure
failed Central Oregon because of who owns it.

### Project Description

Steppe answers the ownership problem directly: a member-governed
Oregon nonprofit operating a verified, ad-free community platform.
Residents verify local residency, pay $4 a month, and get a calm,
chronological space for neighborhood events, mutual exchange, and
binding community votes — with full Spanish access, due-process
moderation, and quarterly public transparency reports. The commitments
that matter — no advertising ever, member-owned data, community control
— are written into our articles of incorporation; surplus revenue
flows to local charities the members choose by ranked-choice vote. The
platform is complete and security-audited as of June 2026, with 35
Redmond neighborhoods mapped and Redmond Compass as our launch partner.

This grant funds the two-year program that turns a launched platform
into rural civic infrastructure. Year one places a part-time community
coordinator in Redmond to build the founding cohort, support volunteer
moderators, and run neighborhood outreach — and carries structured
listening sessions to six rural towns so that expansion, when those
communities petition for it, is shaped by what they actually said. Year
two sustains the coordinator and adds a paid internship pilot: two
Central Oregon students, in technology and community tracks, paid above
minimum wage, finishing with a public showcase — the platform maintained
by the community’s own young people.

### Community Benefit

Direct benefit flows to Redmond’s \~36,000 residents at launch and to
the rural towns in the listening-session circuit. Specific populations
served: Spanish-speaking households (full localization, professionally
reviewed), residents excluded by standard verification systems (PO box
and non-standard-document accommodation with human review), and rural
young people (paid internships). Structural benefit flows to all of
Central Oregon: a permanent, locally-owned alternative to extractive
platforms, with a governance model published openly for any rural
community to adopt.

### Outcomes & Evaluation

  - 200 verified members by mid-2027 (≈0.5% of Redmond) and 100 by end
    of 2026

  - Six rural-town listening sessions completed, findings published

  - Quarterly bilingual transparency reports, on schedule, every quarter

  - First binding community votes held, with turnout and appeal-vs-exit
    rates published

  - Two paid internships completed with public showcase (year two)

All measures run on the platform’s own public health dashboard — funders
read the same numbers members do.

### Sustainability

Member dues fund core operations permanently; this grant funds capacity
that dues cannot yet reach. By the grant’s end, dues at the 200–500
member level carry the coordinator role into the operating budget, and
the internship program transitions to a mix of dues surplus and
dedicated workforce-development funding. By rule, no single funder may
exceed 30% of our annual revenue — a Roundhouse award extends the
commons; it never becomes its dependency.
